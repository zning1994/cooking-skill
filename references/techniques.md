# Techniques

## Core rule

Most home-cooking failures are not seasoning failures first.
They are sequencing, moisture, heat, or overcooking failures.

## 1. Marinating

Use when:

- meat needs tenderness
- flavor needs to go in before the pan
- a coating needs to hold

Basic idea:

- add a little salt or soy for base flavor
- add starch for a light protective coat
- add a little oil to reduce sticking and moisture loss

Good signs:

- slices look lightly glossy, not swimming in liquid
- starch clings in a thin film, not wet sludge

Common mistakes:

- too much liquid -> meat steams instead of sears
- too much starch -> gummy coating
- marinating too long with too much salt -> texture tightens

## 2. Blanching

Use when:

- removing raw smell
- setting shape
- shortening final cook time
- clearing impurities for soups and braises

Good signs:

- water returns to a light boil quickly
- ingredients firm up slightly without fully cooking through

Common mistakes:

- blanching too long -> later cooking becomes mushy
- skipping cold rinse when texture matters

## 3. Stir-frying

Key rule:

The pan order matters more than the full ingredient list.

General order:

1. hot pan
2. oil
3. aromatics
4. protein or hard vegetables
5. soft vegetables
6. sauce near the end

Good signs:

- ingredients sound active, not watery
- pan surface is hot enough that food starts moving and coloring
- vegetables stay bright rather than collapse immediately

Common mistakes:

- overcrowding the pan -> steaming
- adding sauce too early -> watery pan, dull flavor
- weak prep -> still cutting garlic while the pan is already hot

## 4. Braising / red-braising

Use when:

- you want a richer, safer home-cooking result
- ingredients benefit from absorbing sauce
- the dish needs tolerance rather than precision

Key moves:

- color the protein first if possible
- build aroma with aromatics and sauce base
- add liquid after the base is ready
- keep a gentle simmer, not a violent boil

Good signs:

- bubbles are steady but not explosive
- sauce gradually thickens and coats the ingredients
- potatoes or similar vegetables soften from edge inward

Common mistakes:

- too much liquid -> washed-out sauce
- hard boil -> broken texture, muddy taste
- underseasoning early and trying to save it only at the end

## 5. Thickening with starch slurry

Use when:

- the dish needs a glossy, clingy finish
- sauce should coat ingredients instead of pooling

Key rule:

Slurry goes in late, and the dish must be hot enough to activate it.

Good signs:

- sauce turns from thin to lightly glossy in seconds
- it coats the back of a spoon or the ingredients

Common mistakes:

- too much slurry -> pasty finish
- adding slurry into a weak simmer -> cloudy, raw starch feel

## 6. Frying and second frying

Use when:

- texture contrast matters
- you want crisp exterior and stable color

Second frying is useful when:

- the first fry cooks through
- the second fry restores or deepens crispness

Common mistakes:

- oil not hot enough -> greasy coating
- crowding -> temperature crash
- frying once too long -> dark outside, dry inside

## 7. Doneness cues

### Chicken

- surface firms up
- juices run clearer
- near-bone areas are no longer visibly raw

### Beef slices

- color changes quickly from red to just-browned
- edges curl slightly
- stop early for stir-fry; carryover heat finishes the rest

### Eggs

- curds set but still moist
- if they look fully dry in the pan, they are already past ideal

### Potatoes in braise

- chopsticks or a knife enter with light resistance
- edges begin to soften and take on sauce color

### Tomato sauce base

- visible free juice comes out first
- then the liquid thickens slightly and turns more integrated

## 8. Common failures and rescues

### Meat is tough

Likely causes:

- wrong cut direction
- overcooking
- no protective marinade

Fix:

- next time cut against the grain
- marinate lightly with starch and oil
- cook in shorter bursts

### Dish is watery

Likely causes:

- wet ingredients
- crowded pan
- sauce added too early

Fix:

- raise heat and reduce
- remove some liquid if there is too much
- use a small starch slurry only if the dish suits it

### Dish is too salty

Fix options:

- add neutral bulk: potato, tofu, more vegetables, more rice or noodles
- add a little water and rebalance if it is a braise or sauce dish
- do not blindly add sugar unless sweetness belongs in the dish

### Dish lacks aroma

Likely causes:

- aromatics added too late or not bloomed
- heat too weak
- protein never got color

Fix:

- bloom scallion, ginger, garlic earlier
- let the protein or sauce base develop a little color

### Coating falls off

Likely causes:

- too much surface moisture
- batter or starch layer too thick
- pan disturbed too early

Fix:

- dry the surface better
- coat thinner
- let it set before turning

## 9. Useful substitutions

- no cooking wine -> ginger, scallion, and blanching can handle part of the job
- no oyster sauce -> a little sugar plus light soy can cover some body, but not all of it
- no starch -> reduce sauce longer, or use less liquid and accept a thinner finish
- no dark soy -> use regular soy carefully; do not chase color at the expense of salt balance
- no wok -> use a wide pan, smaller batches, and lower expectations for wok hei

## 10. Practical rule of thumb

If you must choose, choose:

- better sequencing over more ingredients
- better heat control over more sauce
- better texture over more complexity

A simpler dish cooked in the right order beats a “big” dish cooked in chaos.
